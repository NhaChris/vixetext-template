# Abstract

Learning programming logic is among the main difficulties faced by students entering computing programs, and introductory algorithm courses account for a large share of failures and dropout in the field. Gamification initiatives designed to address this problem, however, are often limited to external rewards such as points, badges and leaderboards, without changing the nature of the programming activity itself. This research project proposes the development of CodeMage, a turn-based combat game in which building algorithms is the core mechanic: instead of selecting ready-made abilities, the player programs the spells of a character in a restricted subset of the Lua language, written in an editor embedded in the game and executed in an isolated environment (*sandbox*). Making progress requires decomposing each problem, understanding why a solution failed, debugging it and generalizing it, since opponents have immunities that force the player to rethink their strategies. The research is applied in nature, exploratory and descriptive, and adopts Design Science Research as its method. The planned path includes a literature review on computational thinking, gamification and Problem-Based Learning, the analysis of related games and tools, the design of the game architecture and the formalization of the language subset as a context-free grammar. The expected outcome is a distributable artifact that can serve as a complementary teaching resource in introductory programming courses and show that the code written by the learner can be the center of the game experience, rather than a chore to be sweetened with rewards.
